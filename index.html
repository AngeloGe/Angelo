About Me

My journey began with a question that felt almost too simple to ask: Why does a master roaster know exactly when the leaves are ready — and can science explain what their hands already feel?

Growing up surrounded by Chinese tea culture, I was captivated by West Lake Longjing — a green tea whose flavor has been refined over centuries. But the roasting process remained stubbornly intuitive. Temperature, timing, and touch were passed down as craft, not as data. I decided to change that. I turned the tea wok into my laboratory, designing controlled experiments that measured what tradition had always sensed.

That project became more than a science fair entry — it became a bridge between ancient heritage and modern food science. Today, I carry the same mindset into nutrition, community leadership, and every question I pursue: respect the wisdom of the past, but never stop asking whether the numbers agree.

Featured Research

Independent Research · 2025–2026

A Quantitative Analysis of Processing Variables in Longjing Tea Roasting: Optimizing Sensorial Quality and Polyphenol Retention

The Core Question: How do exact roasting temperatures (170°C, 190°C, 210°C) and durations (3.5 min, 5.3 min, 7.1 min) interact to mathematically alter the chemical components (tea polyphenols) and sensory profiles of green tea?

Background

West Lake Longjing carries over a thousand years of tea-making heritage, yet its roasting remains largely intuition-driven. At a Longjing ecological tea garden in Manjuelong Village, Hangzhou, I designed a reproducible experiment to quantify how wok temperature and roasting time shape both sensory quality and polyphenol retention.

Methodology

Two-factor completely randomized experimental design with 9 treatment groups (G1–G9). Data analyzed via descriptive statistics, Two-Way ANOVA, and Multiple Linear Regression (OLS) using Python (Statsmodels).

  • Two-Factor ANOVA
  • OLS Regression
  • Python · Statsmodels
  • Blind Sensory Evaluation

Key Findings

P = 0.0173

Roasting duration significantly impacts polyphenol retention (P < 0.05) — time is the primary driver of chemical change during processing.

14×

Statistically validated the traditional rule of "High Temperature, Short Time" — time's negative effect is ~14× greater than temperature's positive effect.

190°C · 5.3 min

The mathematical golden window — G5 scored highest overall (92.5/100) with strong polyphenol retention (8.0/10).
